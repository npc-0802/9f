An essay on building science · No. 03

Healthy building decisions have high-stakes consequences in data centers.

From the laboratories of 9 Foundations · Reporting on the air, the equipment, the people downwind. Filed for investors, operators, and the engineers who choose the filters.

Server halls are the largest new building type of the decade. They are projected to consume 10% of all U.S. electricity by 2030. And yet most of the choices that determine how much of that energy is wasted, how much equipment lasts, and how much air gets pushed back into the neighborhoods around them are still made by feel.

We spent the better part of a year modeling every existing and planned data center in the Commonwealth of Virginia — 412 sites, one of the densest hyperscale clusters on Earth — to find out what those small decisions actually cost. The number that came back was larger than we expected.

I · The Stakes

A building is a question disguised as a box.

For most of the twentieth century, the question buildings asked was a simple one: are the people inside comfortable? The healthy-buildings field, born in part out of Harvard’s public-health tradition, complicated the question. It pointed out that the air people breathe at work governs how well they think, how often they get sick, and how long they live. The building, in other words, is not the container of the work. It is part of the work.

A data center asks the same question on different terms. The occupants are not people; they are racks of equipment that fail predictably when their environment drifts. But the consequences leak outward all the same — through the meter, through the grid, through the chimney of a peaking plant somewhere two counties over, through the lungs of a child who lives near that plant. The building is still part of the work. The work is now electricity, climate, and air.

1 The healthy-buildings framework was codified in the book Healthy Buildings: How Indoor Spaces Can Make You Sick — or Keep You Well (Harvard University Press), coauthored by 9F’s chief executive.

II · The Case Study

There is no such thing as a generic Virginia data center.

Virginia carries more data center load than any other state in the country, and most of any sub-national territory in the world. Drive Route 28 north out of Manassas and you can see the cooling towers from the highway. The story most reporters tell about that landscape is one of scale — the gigawatts, the substations, the cornfields traded for concrete.

What we found, when we built the inventory, was variation. Two buildings half a mile apart, both labeled “data center” on the assessor’s map, will differ by an order of magnitude on the metrics that determine their energy and air-quality outcomes. Floor area. Ceiling height. The fraction of IT load handled by air versus liquid. The number of filters in the recirculation loop. The variation is not noise. The variation is the asset.

III · The Archetype

This is how statewide data becomes building-level insight.

An inventory is only as useful as the buildings you can model from it. We translate the parameters of each site — geometry, HVAC topology, filter counts, operating schedules — into a self-consistent representative archetype. The archetype below is anonymized, drawn statistically from the Northern Virginia cluster. It is not a specific address.

Outside, particulate matter arrives at the building boundary in concentrations governed by weather, traffic, and the regional combustion economy. Inside, after passing through two filter banks — a ventilation stage and a recirculation stage — the air is some fraction of what it was. The mechanics are simple. The economic consequences are not.

“The work of the next decade is not building more data centers. It is choosing well inside the ones we are already building.”

IV · FilterStudio

Small filtration choices, outsized consequences.

Two filters can have the same MERV rating and produce wildly different bills. One holds more dust before it begins to choke airflow; the other does not. One forces the fan to push harder against a rising pressure drop; the other does not. The MERV label, which buyers often treat as the spec, says nothing about either of those things over time.

FilterStudio is our answer to that gap. It models filter performance against real building archetypes, over months and years of operation, and translates the difference into the four currencies that decision-makers actually use: energy, climate damages, public-health damages, and dollars.

V · MATRIX

Operational changes have measurable energy implications. Test them before you build them.

Building upgrades are expensive enough that most teams plan them in the imperative voice: we will retrofit the envelope; we will retune the setpoints; we will swap out the chillers. The trouble is that any single intervention interacts with the others in ways that a spreadsheet cannot anticipate. The combined savings are rarely the sum of the parts.

MATRIX is a physics-based simulation engine that takes a real building, builds a digital sister of it, and runs thousands of scenarios in minutes. The output is not a recommendation; it is a decision surface — a map of how energy intensity responds to two or three knobs at once, with the optimum visible. Operators see, before they spend, where the floor is. We have measured combined interventions reach up to 2% annual energy savings on the representative archetype.

VI · H.E.A.A.L.

Real-time building data closes the gap between modeled performance and the air actually in the room.

A model is a hypothesis. The building either confirms it or it does not. H.E.A.A.L. is the field-tier instrumentation we deploy when an operator wants to verify what the simulation predicted — low-cost sensors, calibrated against reference monitors, sampling the conditions that matter to equipment health and to the people in and around the facility.

Every metric matters twice: once for the servers that need stable operating conditions, once for the workers, neighbors, and downwind communities whose lungs are also a kind of intake. Drift is visible before it becomes downtime. Modeled performance becomes observed performance. The conversation moves from we think to we know.

VII · The Method

How a kilowatt becomes a life.

Energy saved is not, by itself, an answer to a question anyone in public health asks. The translation from a kilowatt-hour at the meter to a damage avoided downwind requires a bridge. We use Harvard’s Co-Benefits of the Environment tool — CoBE — to do that work. CoBE attributes the marginal electricity savings to the marginal generation it displaces, on the regional grid, and converts that displacement into emissions, then into the public-health and climate cost those emissions would have imposed.

The result is a number an operator can quote to a regulator and a neighbor in the same sentence. The same percentage point of energy savings reads as dollars on a P&L, as tons of CO2 avoided, and as the morbidity and mortality cost not paid.
