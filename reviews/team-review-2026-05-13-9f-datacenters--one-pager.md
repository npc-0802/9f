# Team review — 9 Foundations Data Centers landing page — One-pager

**Date:** 2026-05-13
**Slug:** 9f-datacenters
**Source:** team-review-2026-05-13-9f-datacenters.md

## What the review says, in one page

The page is visually credible and structurally sound — it earns a meeting on the strength of Joe Allen's Harvard credentials and a genuinely proprietary 412-site Virginia data center inventory that no competitor in this category appears to have. All four reviewers agree the bones, the type system, the section spine, and the dark-technical brand register all work. The problem is that the surfaces meant to *demonstrate* 9F's analytical rigor — the FilterStudio comparison, the MATRIX heatmap, the H.E.A.A.L. dashboard — currently decorate where they should teach. The FilterStudio A/B/Compare toggle changes nothing but pillar opacity. The MATRIX scenario chips shift cell values uniformly by a few percent, so the heatmap reads as static across all four scenarios. The H.E.A.A.L. sensor grid and building score reshuffle randomly on every floor toggle. For an audience that is implicitly engineers and CFOs, the first interaction reveals the dashboards as theater.

The second cross-cutting finding is that the page is a brochure, not a tool. There is exactly one CTA — a mailto to "Talk to an expert" — and zero client evidence: no logos, no named or anonymized case studies, no pricing, no engagement model, no leave-behind PDF, no portfolio-screening offer, no calendar link. From a buyer's seat (Apollo PM) and from BD's seat (9F's revenue lead), both reviewers independently conclude the page elevates perceived rigor but leaks at exactly the points a procurement officer or champion needs to advance internally. The Virginia inventory — the page's strongest asset — currently shows hover state on PUE and IT load instead of asking the visitor "is your site in our model?" which is the conversion surface staring everyone in the face.

The third cross-cutting finding is positioning around H.E.A.A.L. and MATRIX. Apollo flags H.E.A.A.L. as wearing office-building IAQ language (TVOC, CO₂, occupant comfort) when the data-center buyer cares about ASHRAE TC 9.9 envelope drift, particulate ingress on electronics, corrosion risk, and hot-aisle anomalies. MATRIX's "up to 2% annual energy savings · ~$40k" is below the noise floor of competing optimization pitches and undersells the product; either the headline number is sandbagged or MATRIX is the weakest of the three offerings and should not be the middle act. These aren't surface problems — they're product-narrative problems.

Visual craft has one quick-win cluster: the cyan gradient is overused (six different "this is important" treatments compete), numerics aren't running on tabular figures (so the count-ups wobble width during animation), and the three product charts speak three different chart dialects. A token-level CSS pass — `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums`, reserved gradient usage, shared chart-axis vocabulary, hover-state semantics restricted to interactive elements — lifts the page a half-tier in perceived craft without any redesign.

## Net assessment

This is a pre-launch v0.9 marketing surface for a thought-leadership consultancy dressed as a product page. It is good enough to send to a prospect today *as a credibility prop* and earn a discovery call on the strength of Joe Allen plus 412 sites. It is not yet good enough to (a) pre-qualify a buyer on cost, scope, geography, or product fit; (b) survive the second meeting where procurement asks for client references and SOWs; or (c) prove that 9F is shipping software products rather than selling consulting hours. The single most important truth from this review: every interactive surface on the page is currently *less credible* than the static copy around it, and that ratio has to invert before launch.

## What next sprint should focus on

- **Make every interactive surface honest.** Wire the FilterStudio A/B toggle to redraw both charts and swap pillar values; rebuild the MATRIX heatmap as a decision surface with annotated best-cell, color legend, and scenarios that visibly reshape the gradient; seed the H.E.A.A.L. sensor grid and building score with three deterministic per-floor profiles. Currently the strongest case for "9F is just smart consultants" is the page's own theatricality — this fix block neutralizes that.
- **Replace one CTA with three offer-shaped CTAs.** "Request the Virginia inventory brief (PDF)" / "Screen my portfolio" / "Pilot H.E.A.A.L. on one building" — each matches a different buyer intent and budget authority, and each gives the BD function a closeable loop with measurable conversion. Drop the mailto in favor of a calendar link on the primary CTA. This is the single largest BD-utility delta available.
- **Get one named or anonymized client engagement onto the page.** "A top-5 colocation operator" / "an 80MW NOVA campus" with a real before/after — even anonymized — converts the page from thought-piece to product. Pair with at least an institutional logo bar (Harvard, partner research bodies) so the credibility section isn't carried entirely by Joe's name.
- **Reframe H.E.A.A.L. around equipment-risk language and decide MATRIX's narrative weight.** Swap TVOC/CO₂/occupant tiles for ASHRAE TC 9.9 envelope drift, particulate ingress, corrosion risk, hot-aisle anomalies. Either lead MATRIX with the $16M+ portfolio-scale number or demote it inside FilterStudio. These are product-narrative changes, not copy changes.
- **Run the craft-token pass on CSS.** Add `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums` to all numeric classes; reserve `--grad-1` for one element per section; restrict `.card:hover` border-cyan to a `.card--interactive` modifier; build a single chart-axis vocabulary and re-render the MATRIX heatmap and H.E.A.A.L. sparklines against it. ~30 lines of CSS, half-tier lift in perceived craft.
