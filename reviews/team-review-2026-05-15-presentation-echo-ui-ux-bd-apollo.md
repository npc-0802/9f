## ux_agency_lead — UX (flow / friction / motion)

### 1. Overall verdict

The recognition pass is doing real work. FilterStudio, in particular, now reads as a structured case-study product rather than a chart gallery — the case-strip → site characteristics → KEY INSIGHT lane → TBO sequence is the right rhythm, and an audience who watched the deck will land in this section and feel "yes, this is the same story." The H.E.A.A.L. console strip is genuinely effective recognition chrome. Where it falls down is in two predictable places: the 30/7-day tab pair is not just label-only — it's actively misleading because the chart axis still says "00:00 … 23:00," and the FilterStudio TBO stack swapped the deck's stacked-row anatomy for a single horizontal lane row that loses the comparison architecture the deck audience memorized. Recognition payoff is mostly there; the remaining issues are about not undoing it with wrong-axis controls and under-formatted compare tiles.

### 2. Top strengths

- **FilterStudio case-strip + Site Characteristics block** (`index.html:277-289`, `app.js:1158-1183`). The "FILTERSTUDIO · Data Center A · Iron Mountain | 1 Year Period" eyebrow is exactly what page-05/page-11 use; the two-column Location/Building/System briefing under it mirrors page-04/page-10 cleanly. This is the strongest piece of recognition work on the page — an audience member will recognize it on first scroll.
- **KEY INSIGHT #1/#3/#6 framing on chart captions** (`index.html:306, 314, 322`). The two-line caption with a colored lane tag + display-typography title is the deck's exact "KEY INSIGHT #N · Lane" treatment from page-05/06/07, and the chart left-rules pick up the lane color (`styles.css:936-945`). It reads as an annotation, not as decorative chrome — see issue list for one caveat.
- **H.E.A.A.L. operator console strip** (`index.html:481-503`, `styles.css:1771-1836`). Live-data pulse on the left, TRH/CO₂/PM₂.₅/TVOC tabs across, BLDGID badge on the right — slide 18 lifted into web grammar without copy-paste. The 2px accent top-border on the console wrapper sells "operator dashboard chrome" in one glance.
- **Inventory side-panel groupings** (`app.js:490-513`, `styles.css:770+`). "Location Information / Building Information / System Characteristics" sub-headers turn what used to be one undifferentiated dt/dd list into a briefing card. Cheap change, big recognition lift.
- **TBO lane color vocabulary** (`styles.css:1164-1183`, `app.js:1031-1080`). Performance royal, Health purple, Energy pink-red, Society yellow, Dollars green — that's the deck's exact five-category palette, and now both the chart left-rules and the TBO tiles speak it. The color logic itself is right; the layout is what I'd argue with (issue 3).

### 3. Top issues (ranked by impact)

1. **The 30 days / 7 days tabs are dishonest, not scaffolding** (`app.js:2274-2284`, `index.html:493-495`). When you click "30 days" the timeseries title updates to "H.E.A.A.L. Temperature · Timeseries · Last 30 days" but the chart underneath still has hour ticks at `00:00, 06:00, 12:00, 18:00, 23:00` (`app.js:2106-2119`) and the heatmap subhead still says "sensors × 24 h" (`index.html:553`). That's not a forgivable scaffold — that's a control that contradicts the axis it sits on. An operator who reads the title and looks at the axis loses trust in everything else on the surface. The brief asks if this reads as honest scaffolding or as a broken control: as built, broken. Two fixes are acceptable; see recommendation 1.

2. **The TBO compare strip lost the deck's row anatomy** (`styles.css:1102-1110`, `app.js:1031-1080`). Slide 8 and slide 14 stack the five categories as **rows**, with Filter A and Filter B values **side-by-side within each row**. You can sweep a finger down the rows and visually compare. The site instead lays out 6 vertical lane tiles across one horizontal grid. That collapses the comparison into a "winner string" inside each tile (`H&V saves 38 Pa pressure drop`) — which is fine on its own, but loses the deck's "look down the column to compare like-for-like" gesture. An audience who memorized the deck's TBO grid will not recognize this as the same artifact.

3. **TBO at Month 0 of autoplay reads as broken** (`app.js:1018-1024`). When the year scrubber auto-starts in viewport (`app.js:1356-1368`) state.month begins at 0. At month 0: pA = pB = 65 Pa → "H&V Nanowave saves 0 Pa pressure drop"; eA = eB = 0 MWh → "H&V Nanowave saves 0 MWh / year"; total = $0/y → "H&V Nanowave annual upside $0 /y total." Six tiles all reading 0 in the first second of arrival is the worst possible first impression for the recognition surface. The PM₂.₅ tile shows a tiny noise delta which is even worse — it implies precision the system doesn't have at month 0.

4. **The "FILTERSTUDIO · Data Center A · Iron Mountain | 1 Year Period" strip and the existing eyebrow + section H2 stack too much chrome before the user gets to a chart** (`index.html:236-289`). Section eyebrow "02 · FilterStudio" → H2 "Select filters that are right for your building" → lede paragraph → fs__controls row (3 control groups) → fs__case-strip → fs__site briefing block → intent block → charts. That's seven horizontal bands before the first chart. The case-strip and site characteristics are individually valuable recognition cues, but stacking them under the existing eyebrow + lede + controls means the audience scrolls past three "this is a section" headers before getting to insight #1. The deck shows each frame as a single slide; on the web they're all stacked.

5. **The MATRIX chapter strip is fine but redundant under the existing section head** (`index.html:382-388`). "03 · MATRIX" eyebrow + "Simulate before you build" H2 + lede + then "MATRIX · Grid Interactive Buildings | Portfolio · Selected building · Configuration matrix · Financial case" chapter strip. The chapter strip is doing useful workflow-naming work for the user — but it duplicates the MATRIX word the section eyebrow already provided. Either the chapter strip absorbs the eyebrow role, or it gets demoted to a workflow nav (without the MATRIX wordmark). As built it reads like the page is anxious about whether you saw it was MATRIX.

6. **"KEY INSIGHT #1 · Performance" eyebrow lands well, but the choice of numbers (#1, #3, #6) without #2, #4, #5 raises a question the surface can't answer.** The numbering matches the deck's slide numbering, which is exactly the recognition the brief asks for — for someone who saw the deck. For someone who didn't (e.g., a colleague who got the take-home forwarded to them), "#1, #3, #6" reads like a missing #2 and they wonder where it went. A one-line "(performance / health / energy — three of the six insight categories the deck presents)" anywhere in the section would close the loop. Minor, but it's exactly the kind of cue that costs you in the 20% of recipients who didn't watch the talk.

7. **The 1-year scrubber moves under the user during reading** (`app.js:1251-1273`, `1356-1368`). The IntersectionObserver auto-starts the year scrub at 0.35 threshold the first time FilterStudio enters viewport. So the user lands, starts reading the eyebrow + H2, and the chart underneath is already animating, the readouts are already changing, and the TBO tiles are mutating. That's motion competing with comprehension. The deck-recognition pass needs the static state to land first; autoplay should kick in either after a Read pause (~2 sec dwell with no scroll) or only on click. Right now the user has to chase a moving target before they've finished orienting.

### 4. Highest-leverage recommendations

1. **Pick one of two honest fixes for the 30/7-day tabs.** Either (a) hide the range tabs entirely until the data layer can back them with a real 30-day series — recognition is not worth a contradicting axis; or (b) keep the tabs and label the axis ambiguously ("hours" → "selected window") plus drop the `00:00 / 23:00` ticks and replace with relative tick marks (`–30d / –20d / –10d / now` when 30d, `–7d / –4d / –1d / now` when 7d). Option (b) is cheap — you're not changing the underlying series, just relabeling the ticks. Either works; the current state does not.

2. **Re-lay the TBO compare strip as five rows, not six lanes.** Mirror page-08 directly: five horizontal rows (Performance, Health, Energy, Society, Dollars), each row split into a Filter A column and a Filter B column. The "winner / saves" actor copy belongs in a third "delta" column on the right of each row, not inside each tile. This is the single change that would most strengthen recognition — and it would also fix the Month-0 problem because zero-deltas would render as legible "0.00 vs 0.00" cells rather than "saves 0 Pa pressure drop."

3. **Set state.month default to 12 visually + hold autoplay until first user interaction or 2-second dwell.** The whole-year terminal state is the most legible first impression — it matches the deck (which shows annual results) and it doesn't surface near-zero deltas. The autoplay can still happen, but trigger it on a deliberate user gesture (click the timer, or click any case/filter chip) rather than on intersection. The intersection-triggered autoplay (`app.js:1356-1368`) is fighting the user's reading.

4. **Consolidate the FilterStudio header stack.** Roll the section eyebrow + H2 + the case strip into a single header band: `02 · FilterStudio` left-justified, then `Data Center A · Iron Mountain · 1 Year Period` right-justified on the same line, then H2 underneath. That reclaims ~120px of vertical chrome and gets the audience to the charts faster on first arrival. Do the same for MATRIX: the chapter strip can replace the lede position if the lede shrinks, instead of stacking under both.

5. **Make the timer label honest about cumulative-vs-instantaneous.** "Month 12 / 12" reads as a point in time, but the Energy series is cumulative across the year and Pressure is end-of-year peak. A two-word qualifier — "Month 12 of 12 · cumulative" or "Year-end · Month 12 / 12" — would close the readability gap. Cheap.

### 5. What I would watch next

- The 30/7-day tabs after recommendation 1 lands — does an operator with the deck still in their head accept the relabeled axis, or do they expect the actual series to swap?
- TBO compare strip after the row-anatomy fix — does the H&V vs Camfil mental model survive a 60-second hover test, or do users still need the actor sentence inside each cell to know who wins?
- The autoplay decision — if you hold the scrubber until intent, does the case-study story still land in the first 5 seconds, or did the autoplay-on-arrival pattern have a recognition value we'd lose?
- The "KEY INSIGHT #1, #3, #6" decision once the section gets shared outside the deck audience. If anyone asks "where's #2" in a customer call, that's a signal to add the explainer line.

### 6. Scorecard

- **Usability: 6/10.** The 30/7-day misalignment, the autoplay-on-intersection collision with reading, and the Month-0 zero-tile state are three discrete friction points an operator will hit in the first 10 seconds. Take those out and this is an 8.
- **Clarity: 7/10.** The eyebrow + KEY INSIGHT + lane-color discipline is clear; the TBO compare grid is the one place where the deck's anatomy got translated wrong. Layout consolidation pushes this to 8.
- **Trust: 5/10.** The 30/7-day tabs alone cost two trust points — the moment the user notices the axis doesn't match the title, they question every other label on the page. Fix that one issue and trust jumps to 8.

## ui_brand_systems_lead — UI (typography / spacing / brand)

### 1. Overall verdict

The recognition pass has a real spine — the FilterStudio case-strip, the Site Characteristics block, the KEY INSIGHT #N tags, the operator-console strip, and the MATRIX chapter strip all carry deck DNA you can point at. From across the room, a presentation attendee will recognize the surface. **At pixel range, the recognition partially collapses on one specific axis: filter color encoding.** The deck's loudest visual signature is the H&V-green / Camfil-blue series pairing, and the site charts those same filters in Royal-blue / Sky-blue. The site also still leans heavily on its own typography voice (Avenir/Jost, mono captions) when the deck leans hard into uppercase display weight, so the echoes read as "translated" rather than "the same artifact." Two new debts are visible — the case-strip and chapter-strip share a near-identical gradient signature so the deck-echo motif becomes its own repeating shape, and the deck's vertical category rail (Overview / Performance / Health / Energy / Dollars) is absent.

### 2. Top strengths

- **KEY INSIGHT #N tag system is clean and consistent.** `styles.css:903-911` mono / 0.66rem / 0.16em tracking matches the deck's chart-eyebrow voice precisely. The left-border lane color (`styles.css:936-945`) couples the tag color to the chart frame in one move — `.fs__chart--performance` border `#0A74D6`, `.fs__chart--health` `#A266C9`, `.fs__chart--energy` `#D85E80` — and these are pulled straight off slide 8/14.

The Performance/Health/Energy/Society/Dollars lane sequence with category-color left rules and lane-tinted values reads as a continuation of slide 8 the moment the user hits the section.

The website draws both filter series in two blues that desaturate against the dark navy ground; without the green, the eye has no quick winner-loser signal and the slide-7 recognition trigger evaporates. Map A → `#0A74D6` Royal and B → `#7CB342` (the same green you already define at `styles.css:944` for the Dollars lane and `styles.css:967` for the case-strip mark). This is one line.
